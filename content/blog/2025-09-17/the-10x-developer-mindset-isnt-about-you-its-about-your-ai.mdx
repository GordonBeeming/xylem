For about 10 years, I'd been promising my wife, [Tiani Beeming](https://tianibeeming.com), that I'd build her a new portfolio website. Like many personal projects, it was one of those "I'll get to it eventually" things that never quite happened.

Then I finished it in about five hours.

Not through a sudden burst of productivity or a new JavaScript framework. I stopped being the "doer" and became the "orchestrator," directing a team of AI agents to do the heavy lifting. This project is what really solidified a new idea for me.

## Redefining the "10x Developer"

Let's be honest, the term "10x developer" can have a negative feeling around it. For a long time, I felt the same way. It often brings to mind a lone genius who just smashes out more code than everyone else. But I've been thinking of it differently lately: as a **"10x Developer Mindset."**

To me, this mindset has two core parts:

1.  **Enabling Your Peers:** As an individual contributor, it's about uplifting the team around you. A 10x mindset means you're a force multiplier, enabling more "X" from your peers, not just yourself.
2.  **Leveraging AI Effectively:** In isolation, especially with the rise of AI, it's about your proficiency in using the right AI tool for the right job, whether that's brainstorming, writing a new feature, or doing a massive refactor.

This borrows from DevOps principles and a Growth Mindset. It's about collaboration, continuous improvement, and multiplying effectiveness across a system, not just individual output.

This story is about how I put that second part, leveraging AI, into practice.

## The Frustration with "Prototype" AI

For those of you who know me, you'll know that I am *not* a UI guy. My design sense probably peaked with the gun-metal grey of Windows Forms. Function over form, always.

A big part of that 10-year delay was the pressure to create something that actually looked modern and professional for Tiani's portfolio. That's a high bar for a backend-focused developer, which is why I was so excited by the promise of AI UI generators.

But my initial attempts were frustrating. You've probably seen the tools that promise to build an app from a prompt. I tried v0 and got a cool-looking UI, but I couldn't get the code to actually work in my project. I tried B0lt, and the exported code wouldn't even run locally. All prototype, no product.

Then I tried a new workflow.

## Step 1: The AI as Product Manager (Gemini)

Instead of just diving in, I treated Gemini as my Product Manager. A good project starts with a good plan, and I didn't just give it a one-liner. I had a detailed back-and-forth to build out a real specification for Tiani's site.

We clarified details about the portfolio gallery, the filter labels, and the contact page. The result was a solid, consolidated spec. The better the input, the better the output.

## Step 2: The AI as UI Designer (GitHub Spark)

With the spec in hand, I went over to GitHub Spark. I didn't summarize the plan. I pasted the *entire requirement document* from Gemini directly into the prompt.

What came back genuinely surprised me.

<Figure key="/images/spark-generation.png" src="/images/spark-generation.png" alt="A screenshot of the GitHub Spark UI showing a beautifully generated portfolio website based on the spec from Gemini." width="0" height="0" caption="This was the 'wow' moment. A complete, great-looking starting point in seconds." />

In under 5 minutes, I had a good-looking, functional React frontend that I could push directly to a new GitHub repository. Not a flimsy prototype — a solid foundation I could actually build on.

## Step 3: The Critical Refactor - Unlocking Parallel Work

So I have a great starting point, but it's all in one giant `App.tsx` file. A monolithic structure is a roadblock, and this is something I've learned from my recent experiments with AI agents, like the ones I [covered in my last post](https://gordonbeeming.com/blog/2025-09-16/my-ai-powered-workflow-building-a-full-stack-app-with-chatgpt-and-github-copilot). A specific, well-defined task gives an agent like GitHub Copilot something real to work with. A vague prompt gives you vague results.

My first step wasn't to hack at the code myself. I went back to Gemini and asked it to write a PBI for the task: "Refactor the application from a single monolithic component into a true multi-page app structure using React Router DOM."

With that focused PBI in hand, I created a new GitHub issue and assigned it directly to Copilot.

Better instructions lead to better outcomes." />

This step was the key that unlocked the next, most powerful part of the workflow.

## Step 4: The Human/AI Parallel Workflow

Now that the codebase was modular, I could stop working sequentially. I could finally work in parallel. I was no longer the bottleneck, but that didn't mean I was hands-off.

While the AI worked on a feature in its own branch, I was in the codebase myself. Making small styling tweaks, building my mental model of the architecture, getting a feel for the code Spark and Copilot were generating.

At one point I cloned the repository twice on my machine: one directory for the Copilot agent, one for me. Genuine parallel development. That's where the speed came from.

## The Bonus Round: From React POC to Working Blazor

After the portfolio site was live, I wanted to see how far this workflow could stretch. I had another project where Spark had generated a great React proof of concept for a piece of UI, but the target project used Blazor with a standard control library.

I didn't ask Copilot to "convert" the code. I asked it to re-implement the React POC as a fully working Blazor component using the project's control library.

The first pass was decent but not there yet. It took several rounds of review in the Pull Request to get it right. I'd flag a styling issue or a missing piece of logic, the agent would go away and refine it, and we'd go again.

<Figure key="/images/blazor-conversion-pr.png" src="/images/blazor-conversion-pr.png" alt="A screenshot of the GitHub Pull Request showing multiple review sessions and feedback loops to convert a component to Blazor." width="0" height="0" caption="It wasn't a single shot. It took a genuine back-and-forth review process to get the Blazor component perfect." />

This unlocked something I hadn't anticipated. Tools like GitHub Spark are focused on popular frameworks like React, and that's fine. But the React code isn't really the point. The real value is using that fully-formed UI as a blueprint. Prototype in React, hand it to an agent as a detailed spec, get it re-implemented in whatever stack you actually need. It's a good way to bridge the gap between UI design and implementation in your target tech stack.

## The Leadership Parallel: Mentoring Your AI

I was chatting with [Daniel Mackay](https://www.linkedin.com/in/danieljamesmackay/) (again) and we landed on something I keep coming back to. I'm paraphrasing because I don't remember the exact words, but the gist was: working with AI agents feels a lot like mentoring a junior developer.

Think about it. To get the best out of a junior dev, you need:
* Patience — you don't expect their first attempt to be perfect.
* Clear instructions — well-defined tasks, not vague ideas.
* Constructive feedback — review their code, explain the "why", guide them toward a better solution.
* Trust — believe they can learn from that feedback loop.

That's the same skillset you need to work with AI agents effectively. The quality of the outcome reflects your ability to lead. Write clear PBIs (and using another agent to help you write them is a smart move), review the PRs, and have the patience to iterate. I've found this whole process makes you a better technical leader too — you get a lot of reps at giving precise, useful feedback. You have to work *with* your AI, not against it.

## Conclusion: The 10x Orchestrator Mindset

So what does it mean to be a "10x developer" in the age of AI? It's about leverage, not typing speed.

* Use tools like Gemini to think through the what and the why. A good spec is everything.
* Use tools like GitHub Spark and Copilot to handle the how. Delegate the execution.
* Don't wait. While your AI agent is building a feature, plan the next one. Only parallelize to the degree you can keep track of what's going on — like any skill, this gets easier with practice.
* Stay in the loop. You're the quality control. You have the vision.

The role shift is from doer to orchestrator. That's the 10x developer mindset I've landed on.
